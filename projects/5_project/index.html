Mind–Body Interventions and Neural Mechanisms of Healthy Aging (Under Review)

A systematic review and meta-analysis of neuroimaging evidence on meditation, Tai Chi, and yoga in older adults, revealing brain–behavior coupling that supports cognitive and emotional resilience.

Overview

This project presents a systematic review and meta-analysis on how mind–body interventions (MBIs)—including meditation, Tai Chi, and yoga—affect brain structure, function, and cognition in older adults.
Following PRISMA guidelines and a preregistered protocol (OSF: 10.17605/OSF.IO/QE3AM), we synthesized findings from 61 reports (46 RCTs) using structural (sMRI, DTI) and functional (fMRI, EEG, fNIRS, PET) neuroimaging methods.

The meta-analysis revealed convergent neural adaptations across default mode (DMN), frontoparietal/executive control (FPN/ECN), and limbic–hippocampal circuits, accompanied by improvements in global cognition (g = 0.44), memory (g = 0.70), and depression and anxiety reduction (g ≈ 0.5).
Together, these findings highlight that MBIs promote neurocognitive resilience and adaptive brain–behavior coupling during aging.

Key Findings

  1. Neuroplasticity in aging: MBIs consistently increased gray matter volume in the hippocampus, ACC, and insula, and strengthened PCC–mPFC and hippocampal–DMN connectivity supporting memory and self-regulation.
  2. Functional network reorganization: fMRI and EEG studies converged on increased synchronization in the DMN and salience networks, reduced P3 latency, and enhanced N2/ERN amplitudes—markers of improved attentional control.
  3. Brain–behavior coupling: Neural changes correlated with improvements in MoCA, GDS, PSQI, and memory scores, linking functional gains to behavioral outcomes.
  4. Intervention-specific effects:
    • Meditation enhanced fronto-limbic regulation (DLPFC–amygdala coupling).
    • Tai Chi strengthened hippocampal and motor networks linked to sleep and gait performance.
    • Yoga showed right-hippocampal and occipital GMV gains but less consistent behavioral translation.
  5. Meta-analytic outcomes: Pooled effects confirmed significant improvements in depression (g = 0.50), anxiety (g = 0.47), global cognition (g = 0.44), and memory (g = 0.70).
  6. Methodological insights: Fidelity analysis revealed limited reporting and preregistration, underscoring the need for theory-driven, replicable designs in future neuroimaging RCTs.
